Comprehensive analysis of a new X-ray transient. A possible UCXB?

Transients are powerful bright explosions due to which there is a sudden increase in the luminosity of a body that can last weeks to years. The supernova explosions, gamma-ray bursts or accretion in X-ray binaries are some of the events that can cause them.
Swift J0840-3516 is an X-ray binary detected in Feb 2020 by Swift/BAT and was first thought to be a GRB. Zelati et al. suggested the possibility of the source to be an Ultra-compact X-ray source (UCXB) since there was a presence of oxygen lines and no hydrogen lines in the optical spectrum of the source. I used data from multiple X-ray observatories like NICER, NuStar, Swift-XRT, AstroSat to perform a detailed timing and spectral study of the source. There were no pulsations found in the system, and the UV magnitude of 17 suggested this source to be a LMXB system. Moreover, we found out that the photon index of the source evolved from 0.94 to 3.0 (softened) throughout the outburst, which suggested that the LMXB contained a neutron star as an accretor. The simultaneous XRT and UVOT data allowed us to perform the UV versus X-ray flux correlation study to probe the UV emission from the binary. We also did this correlation study for other pre-known UCXB systems to see if there is a remarkable similarity.

  • Key concepts:Transients, UCXB, X-ray binaries.
  • Computational tools:Monte carlo simulations, Pyhon, Heasoft, Ftools, bash

RX J0209.6-7427: A ULP in a binary system.

X-ray binaries are the gravitationally bound system that orbits around a common centre of mass and emits in X-rays. Usually, one of the stars in binary is a compact object (Black hole, Neutron star, or a white dwarf) which accretes matter from the companion star (can be another compact object or a main sequence star).
I actively performed a detailed timing and spectral study of an X-ray binary system RX J0209.6-7427. The luminosity of the source during the outburst has put it into the ultra-luminous X-ray pulsar (ULP) class. The companion star is Be type star, and the binary is classified as the High mass x-ray binary (HMXB). We have found X-ray pulsations originated from the source that indicated an X-ray pulsar as the compact object. Our aim for this source is to study the accretion column of the pulsar using data from AstroSat (SXT and LAXPC). We found that the pulsar is in a spin-up state, and our broadband timing study showed the presence of complex pulse profiles, showing both primary and secondary peaks. These features in the pulse profiles are also strongly dependent on energy.

  • Key concepts:X-ray binaries, Accretion column, ULP, timing and spectral studies.
  • Computational tools:Heasoft, starlink period program, Hendrics, Python, numpy, scipy, matplotlib

X-ray analysis of transient Swift 1658.2-4242.

Black holes are tightly packed stars having masses greater than three solar masses. The gravity near the black hole is so intense (due to its large mass and small size) that even photons couldn't escape. These can be found at the centre of galaxies or in a binary system. Swift J1658.2-4242 is a black hole (candidate) binary system first reported in Feb 2018 using Swift/Bat. This project taught accretion processes in X-ray binaries, including neutron stars and black holes. I also learned data analysis tools and techniques to reduce SXT and LAXPC onboard AstroSat. In particular, we performed detailed broadband (0.3-80 keV) timing and spectral analysis of a black hole X-ray transient, Swift J1658.2-4242 using 20 ks long AstroSat observation. Blackhole binaries often show a large X-ray variability observed in the form of Quasi-periodic oscillations (QPOs) in the power density spectrum. Therefore, we investigated this source and found a low-frequency Type-C QPO around 1.6 Hz that strongly depended on time and energy. We also found that the source was in a hard spectral state (photon index = 1.7) from the spectral study. Our results were consistent with the published results.

  • Key concepts:Black holes, Data pipelining/reduction, Fourie analysis
  • Computational tools:Heasoft, Python

UV photometry of MRK 766 and nearby galaxies.

Mrk 766 is a nearby (z = 0.013) narrow-line Seyfert 1 (NLS1) galaxy (D. J. K. Buisson et al.). NLS1s are thought to be rapidly accreting, relatively low mass AGN, and are distinguished by narrow optical Balmer lines, weak and strong Fe emission (Komossa 2008). The Ultraviolet imaging telescope onboard AstroSat (India's first multi-wavelength mission) was used to perform photometry of a 28 arcmin field of Mrk 766 galaxy. The UVIT image in the NUV filter showed that the MRK766 galaxy had two bright cores at its centre, from which we hypothesise that it might be a merger of two (or more) galaxies. Unfortunately, we could not conclude much due to the lack of redshift data of nearby galaxies. In this process, I also made a catalogue of all the galaxies present in the field and found some new un-catalogued objects.

  • Key concepts:Seyfert galaxy, AstroSat, Group/cluster of galaxies.
  • Computational tools:SExtractor, ds9, Python, Iraf

Geometry of radio pulsars.

Pulsars are fast rotating neutron stars and have a strong magnetic fields. Radio pulsar emits beamed radiation through the process called "magnetic dipole radiation" originating above the magnetic poles. These radiation when crossing the line of sight of observer seen as pulses or flashes. The light curve and pulse profile of the source can reveal a lot about the spin period, emission mechanism, and its rate of slowing down. In this project, I performed detailed timing analyses of B1859+07, and B1647-03 pulsar to find the spin period of these systems.Through the Sky Watch Array Network (SWAN) initiative, I also gained experience with the radio detectors, signal processing, and radio Interferometry.

  • Key concepts:Pulsars, slowing down of a pulsar, magnetic dipole radiation, correlation, convolution, and radio interferometry.
  • Computational tools:Python
